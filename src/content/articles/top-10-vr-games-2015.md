# Top 10 VR Games to Play in 2015

Consumer VR didn't exist yet. The Oculus Rift CV1 was months away. The Vive was a prototype behind glass at trade shows. PlayStation VR was a rumor. And yet — people were playing VR. On development kits, through hacked command lines, via mods held together with community patches and hope. The games that defined VR in 2015 weren't polished launch titles. They were proofs of concept, engineering marvels, and in some cases, happy accidents that happened because someone left a VR mode in the code.

This is the landscape as it actually was on December 31, 2015. Not the marketing version. Not the future that was promised. The ten things you could actually put on a headset and play — ranked by how much they mattered then and how well they hold up now.

---

## #10: Assetto Corsa
**Official Hybrid | Tier: A**

The sim racing community got VR right before almost anyone else. Assetto Corsa's VR support wasn't a bolted-on camera mode — it was a properly integrated cockpit view with head tracking that gave you the spatial awareness every sim racer had been pretending their monitor provided. Lap times dropped. Track positioning became intuitive. The sense of speed transformed from a number on a HUD to something your body actually felt.

The reason it's #10 and not higher is simple: Assetto Corsa's VR was technically available in 2015, but it was rough. Performance demanded serious hardware. The UI wasn't designed for stereoscopic viewing. It was a glimpse of what sim racing in VR could become, not yet the finished article. But that glimpse was enough to sell headsets on its own.

## #9: War Thunder
**Official Hybrid | Tier: B**

War Thunder's VR mode was the free-to-play entry point for anyone who wanted to fly in VR without spending money. The air combat worked — cockpit presence, head tracking for situational awareness, the genuine thrill of spotting a bandit by actually turning your head. Sim Battles were where the headset earned its keep.

The catch was everything else. Ground and naval combat felt awkward in VR. The UI wasn't rebuilt for stereoscopic viewing. Motion controller support was basically nonexistent. And the VR mode had that unmistakable quality of something the developer added because they could, not because they were committed to making it great. Still, in 2015, free VR dogfighting was nothing to dismiss.

## #8: Euro Truck Simulator 2
**Official Hybrid | Tier: B**

The unlikeliest VR champion of 2015. Driving a truck across Europe sounds like the dullest possible use of a headset, and that's exactly why it worked. The low speeds meant minimal motion sickness. The predictable routes meant you could relax into the experience. The sense of actually sitting in a cab, checking mirrors by turning your head, watching the sunset through the windshield — it was meditative in a way that no action game could replicate.

SCS Software supported Oculus dev kits from 2013 onward, making Euro Truck Simulator 2 one of the longest-running VR-compatible titles in existence. In 2015, it was the game you handed to someone who said VR was only for gamers. It proved that presence mattered more than action.

## #7: Project CARS
**Official Hybrid | Tier: C**

Project CARS arrived in 2015 with VR support baked in from development, not patched on after the fact. The community had been testing it throughout early access. By release, it was the VR racing game — the one that demoed headsets at trade shows and made sim racing converts out of sceptics.

The problem was stability. VR performance was inconsistent even on high-end hardware. The UI was hostile to headsets. Patches sometimes broke VR support entirely. When it worked, it was stunning. When it didn't — which was often — it was a frustrating reminder that 2015 VR was still the wild west. The tier reflects the gap between what Project CARS promised and what it reliably delivered.

[Read the full Project CARS VR review](/games/project-cars)

## #6: EVE: Valkyrie
**Official Standalone VR Version | Tier: D**

The flagship. The demo queen. The game that launched with every headset and appeared in every trade show booth. EVE: Valkyrie was the VR poster child in 2015 — a multiplayer space dogfighter with full 360° cockpit, head tracking for target acquisition, and production values that made everything else look like a tech demo.

So why #6 and why D-tier? Because the game underneath the spectacle was thin. The combat loop got repetitive fast. The multiplayer population dwindled. The single-player content was negligible. And within a year, every promise it made about VR's multiplayer future would be eclipsed by games that understood depth better. Valkyrie proved VR could look and feel premium. It didn't prove VR could keep you playing.

## #5: Keep Talking and Nobody Explodes
**Official Standalone VR Version | Tier: B**

The asymmetric party game that defined an entire genre. One person in VR defuses a bomb. Everyone else reads a manual they can't see. The result was chaos, shouting, laughter, and the most accessible VR experience of 2015 — it even ran on Samsung Gear VR, a phone in a plastic holder.

Keep Talking didn't need graphical fidelity or complex physics. It needed trust, communication, and the pressure of a timer. The VR player's inability to see the manual while the manual readers couldn't see the bomb created a tension loop that was immediately compelling to anyone, gamer or not. In a year of tech demos and half-finished mods, this was a finished game that understood VR's social potential before most developers did.

[Read the full Keep Talking and Nobody Explodes VR review](/games/keep-talking-and-nobody-explodes)

## #4: Minecraft VR
**Community Mod (Minecrift) | Tier: A**

The gateway drug. Minecrift — the community mod that added VR support to Minecraft — was arguably the most important flat-to-VR project of 2015, not because it was the most technically impressive, but because it was the most accessible. Everyone already owned Minecraft. Everyone already knew how to play it. Putting on a headset and seeing your blocky world at actual scale for the first time was a conversion moment for thousands of people.

The mod supported head tracking, stereoscopic rendering, and even basic room-scale movement. Performance was surprisingly good on modest hardware because Minecraft's voxel engine was inherently VR-friendly — low-poly geometry and simple textures meant high frame rates were achievable on 2015 hardware. It wasn't official. It wasn't polished. But it proved that flat-to-VR wasn't just possible — it was desirable, and it could reach an audience orders of magnitude larger than any native VR game.

## #3: Alien: Isolation
**Hidden VR Mode | Tier: A**

The best horror game of 2014 happened to have a complete, functioning VR mode hidden in its code. Creative Assembly built full VR support — head tracking, stereoscopic rendering, motion controller input — and then disabled it before release. The community found it, re-enabled it with a command-line flag, and discovered that Sevastopol Station was absolutely terrifying in VR.

This wasn't a hack or a proof of concept. This was a shipped game with a finished VR implementation that the publisher chose not to support. Walking down those corridors with the motion tracker beeping and the alien somewhere above you in the vents — hearing it move through actual 3D space — was the most intense VR experience available in 2015. It was also the most frustrating, because the VR mode had bugs that would never be fixed, UI elements that didn't render correctly, and a developer who officially pretended it didn't exist. The tier reflects the experience when it worked, which was extraordinary.

## #2: Half-Life 2
**Community Mod (Voron/SteamVR) | Tier: S**

The blueprint. Before there was a flat-to-VR community, before there were mod frameworks and injection drivers, there was Half-Life 2 in VR. The community mods that enabled head tracking, motion controls, and room-scale movement through City 17 weren't just impressive — they established the vocabulary that every flat-to-VR project since has built on.

Playing Half-Life 2 in VR in 2015 meant physically ducking behind cover, aiming the Gravity Gun with your actual hands, and experiencing the Combine citadel at a scale that made you genuinely feel small. The pacing, the level design, the environmental storytelling — all of it translated to VR because Valve had designed a game with strong spatial awareness long before VR existed. The S-tier isn't nostalgia. It's recognition that this mod proved flat-to-VR could produce S-tier experiences, and everything since has been chasing that proof.

## #1: Elite Dangerous
**Official Hybrid | Tier: B**

The game that sold VR headsets by itself. Not through marketing or trade show demos — through the simple fact that no other experience in 2015 could make you feel the scale of space the way Elite Dangerous did in VR. Undocking from a station and watching the entrance slot fall away beneath you. Warping to a star and feeling the heat before your eyes adjusted to the light. Landing on planets in the Horizons expansion and standing on the surface of an actual world.

The B-tier rating reflects the compromises: the VR UI was never fully redesigned for headsets, the text was often too small to read comfortably, and Frontier's commitment to VR support has waxed and waned over the years. But in 2015, none of that mattered. Elite Dangerous was the reason to own a DK2. It was the experience you demoed to friends. It was proof that VR wasn't a gimmick — it was a fundamentally better way to exist inside a digital space. Every cockpit sim on this list owes something to what Elite Dangerous proved first.

## Honorable Mentions

**Team Fortress 2** had official VR support from Valve as far back as 2013, making it one of the first flat games to get a VR mode. It worked. It was novel. It was also TF2 — a competitive multiplayer shooter where VR players were at a genuine disadvantage against mouse-and-keyboard opponents. Fun to try, not to live in. [Read the full TF2 VR review](/games/team-fortress-2)

**Dirt Rally** offered VR support that was technically available by late 2015 and genuinely impressive when it ran well. Like Project CARS, the issue was consistency — performance and stability varied wildly. It would mature into one of the best VR racing experiences, but in 2015 it was still finding its footing. [Read the full Dirt Rally VR review](/games/dirt-rally)

**Doom 3 BFG** had the beginnings of VR mod support in 2015 through community efforts that would eventually become the polished experience covered in our review. In 2015, it was promising but rough. [Read the full Doom 3 BFG VR review](/games/doom-3-bfg)

**Lucky's Tale** was the flagship Oculus platformer — a charming, polished 3D platformer that proved VR could work for third-person games. It shipped with every Rift CV1 preorder. It was delightful and forgettable in equal measure, which is why it's here instead of in the top 10. [Read the full Lucky's Tale VR review](/games/luckys-tale)

**DCS World** had VR support from 2015, running on Oculus DK2, and it was already the most realistic combat flight sim you could experience in VR. The barrier to entry — HOTAS hardware, complex keybinds, a learning curve measured in weeks — kept it out of the top 10, but for hardcore sim enthusiasts, it was the real deal even then. [Read the full DCS World VR review](/games/dcs-world)

**Windlands** was one of the first VR games to make swinging through a world feel genuinely exhilarating. The grappling hook movement system produced moments of pure joy that few 2015 VR experiences could match. Motion sickness and limited content kept it out of the main list, but the core mechanic was ahead of its time. [Read the full Windlands VR review](/games/windlands)
